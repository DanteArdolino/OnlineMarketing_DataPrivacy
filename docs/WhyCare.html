Why You Should Care

Leaving a trace online has become commonplace as every time you go online you risk releasing personal information into cyberspace. Personal information has become more and more of a talking point with the rise of an online presence. A Pew Research Institute study found that controlling PI on line is “very important” to 74% of Americans. According to another Pew study, 86% of Americans have taken action to maintain their privacy — deleting cookies, encrypting email, and protecting their IP address.

Overall, you should care because it is ultimately up to the user to be protected online be it their identity or personal information. Because there are a lot of background works at play, it is important to be self serving and understand that there is more than meets the eye when online. There are steps that can be followed in order to prevent potential malicious attempts or even subside the overwhelming presence of data collection

There are laws in place in a patchwork augmentation that protect certain rights online

The Federal Trade Commission Act (FTC)[1914]

  1. regulates unfair or deceptive commercial practices
  2. The FTC is the primary federal regulator in the privacy area and brings enforcement actions against companies
  3. Including failing to comply with posted privacy policies and failing to adequately protect personal information
  4. stay away from free VPNs and try to avoid proxies

Children’s Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA) [1998]

  1. requires certain website and online service providers to obtain verifiable parental consent before collecting, using, or disclosing personal information from minors under the age of 13
  2. requires websites to post an online privacy policy, collect only the personal information necessary, and create and maintain reasonable security measures

Financial Services Modernization Act (GLBA) [1999]

  1. regulates the collection, use, and disclosure of personal information collected or held by financial institutions and requires customer notices and a written information security program

(CAN-SPAM Act) [2003]

  1. governs sending unsolicited commercial email and prohibits misleading header information and deceptive subject lines
  2. requires senders to disclose certain information, include a valid opt-out mechanism, and it creates civil and criminal penalties for violations
